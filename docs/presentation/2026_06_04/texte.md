2. 
    - It is in this scientific context that my thesis is grounded. More specifically, the scientific challenge is the real-time creation of digital twins of organs such as the liver. To address this challenge, the objective of this thesis is the development of a hybrid method combining finite element methods and neural networks for the numerical resolution of PDEs. In fact, finite element methods are accurate methods for which theoretical error estimators are already known, but they can be very costly — hence the interest in coupling them with neural networks (which are fast and can be parametric); here we choose PINNs.
    I just wanted to clarify the context of the thesis, but the work I will present today does not include any medical simulation — it is a preliminary study aimed at evaluating whether the proposed method is promising.
    - The problem we will first focus on is the Poisson problem with homogeneous Dirichlet boundary conditions on a spatial domain Omega. We will study this problem in a parametric setting, meaning that the solution and the source term depend on a certain parameter mu living in the parametric space M.
3. Let us start with PINNs, standing for Physics-Informed Neural Networks. Concretely, what is a PINN? It is a standard neural network (of the dense type) that takes x and mu as inputs. Why do we say "physics-informed"? Because the training does not require data but instead uses the physics of the equation. Concretely, we seek to minimize the following objective with two loss terms: a first one on the residual of the equation integrated over the spatial domain and the parametric domain, and a second one on the Dirichlet boundary condition integrated over the boundary and over the parametric domain. In practice, we discretize these losses by sampling points in our spatial and parametric domains (called collocation points), trying to find the best possible u_θ.
4. 
    - The second approach I mentioned in the introduction is the finite element method. The principle of these approaches is to rewrite the problem in another form, called the variational problem. We then build a mesh of our geometry (with characteristic size h) — here a Cartesian mesh — on which we define a continuous, piecewise polynomial space (of degree k). We then seek to determine a polynomial approximation u_h of the PDE solution u, within the space we just constructed.
    - In practice, to find u_h, we simply solve a linear system where A is a square matrix of a certain size N_dofs. This matrix depends on what we call the degrees of freedom of the space, which are points associated with the mesh cells, and whose number also depends on the chosen polynomial degree k.
    - More precisely, the higher the polynomial degree k and the more refined the mesh (i.e. the smaller the characteristic size h), the more accurate the solution — but also the larger N_dofs.
    - In summary, if we want an accurate solution, we get a large N_dofs and therefore a large linear system to solve, which is costly... and that is precisely what we will try to improve.
